Open Water Safety Checklist: A Family's Complete Guide to Lake, Beach, and River Safety

Most drowning deaths in children over five happen in open water. This printable checklist walks you through the four moments that matter most — what to do before you leave, what to scan when you arrive, how to supervise in the water, and exactly how to respond if something goes wrong.

⚡ Quick Summary
  • Pool swimming skills do not fully transfer to open water — currents, cold, and murky visibility change everything
  • Check weather, water conditions, and flags before anyone enters the water
  • Coast Guard-approved life jackets in boats, docks, and moving water — always, for every child
  • One Water Watcher at all times, no phone, no alcohol, rotating every 15–30 minutes
  • Rip current rule: swim parallel to shore, not against the pull. Teach every child before you go.

The gap between pool swimming and open water is wider than most parents realize. According to the CDC, children ages 5 to 14 drown in natural water settings (lakes, rivers, oceans) more often than in pools. The reason is not that kids are worse swimmers in open water — it is that open water adds variables pools never have: currents, temperature swings, murky visibility, uneven bottoms, and distance that fools the eye.

A child who can swim a confident freestyle lap at the neighborhood pool may panic in a lake when their feet cannot touch, the water is colder than expected, and the shore looks further than it did from the dock.

This guide — and the free printable checklist — walks you through the four moments that matter most for open water family trips. It draws on guidance from the U.S. Coast Guard, the American Red Cross, the CDC, NOAA, the National Drowning Prevention Alliance, and the American Academy of Pediatrics.

Before You Leave Home

Open water days are won or lost in the kitchen the night before. The prep you do at home decides what happens at the water.

Pre-Trip Prep

  • Check the weather and water conditions. Pull the hour-by-hour forecast, tide chart (beaches), river flow rate (NOAA for rivers), and local lake advisory. Cancel or reroute if thunderstorms are forecast within four hours or a small-craft advisory is active.
  • Pack a Coast Guard-approved life jacket for every child. Type II or Type III, properly fitted. Water wings, puddle jumpers, and inflatable toys are not life jackets and do not prevent drowning.
  • Dress children in bright, high-contrast swimwear. Neon yellow, orange, and pink remain visible underwater. Blue, green, white, and black disappear — see our guide to swimsuit color and water safety.
  • Pack a dry bag with phone, ID, and keys. Your phone must be dry and charged when you need it to call 911.
  • Bring a throwable rescue aid. A rescue tube, throw bag, or pool noodle extends your reach and gives a distressed swimmer something to grab without you entering the water.
  • Assign the first Water Watcher before you leave. One adult, no alcohol, no phone, rotating every 15–30 minutes. Write names and shifts on the back of the checklist.

The life jacket question is worth its own line. The U.S. Coast Guard reports that approximately 85 percent of recreational boating drowning victims were not wearing a life jacket. The correlation is so strong that the single biggest thing you can do to prevent an open water drowning is insist on Coast Guard-approved life jackets for every child on boats, docks, and moving water — no exceptions, no "just this once."

Arrival Hazard Scan

The first 10 minutes at the water are for adults, not kids. Unload, hydrate, sunscreen, and do a full hazard sweep before anyone steps into the water. Rushing this step is how avoidable drownings happen.

Locate lifeguards — or mark your location. If there are lifeguards, note their stand. If there are not, pin your exact entry point in your maps app so you can relay it to 911 in seconds. Every minute saved on location matters.

Read the flags and signs. Beach flag colors are standardized in the U.S.: green (low hazard), yellow (moderate hazard — use caution), red (high hazard — no children in the water), double red (water closed), and purple (dangerous marine life like jellyfish or sharks). A red flag is not a suggestion. If the flag is red, the beach is not open for your children today.

Scan the beach for rip currents. Rip currents are narrow channels of water flowing quickly away from shore. Look for channels of churning, choppy water; areas with noticeable differences in water color; lines of foam or debris moving steadily seaward; and breaks in the incoming wave pattern. If you see any of those signs, move your family to a different part of the beach.

Check river current and strainers. Rivers hide sudden deep drops, submerged logs, and "strainer" branches — tree limbs under or over the surface that can trap swimmers. Never let children wade past knee-deep in moving water without adults in the water with them.

Walk the shoreline for hazards. Glass, fishing hooks, sharp rocks, jellyfish, and blue-green algae. In lakes, algae blooms that look like blue-green paint slicks on the surface are toxic. Do not let children swim or dogs drink where algae is visible.

Mark a visible boundary for each child. Point to a physical landmark — a buoy, a dock post, a flagpole. Kids who swim past a visible marker get called back immediately, before they get out of reach. Distance judgment fails fast in open water.

Active Supervision Rules

Once the family is in the water, the rules are simple but non-negotiable.

One designated Water Watcher at all times. Eyes on the water, within arm's reach of non-swimmers, no phone, no book, no alcohol, no side conversations. Rotate in 15- to 30-minute shifts on a timer. If you want a printable Water Watcher card to hand off, use our Water Watcher duty card.

Touch supervision for non-swimmers. Infants, toddlers, and weak swimmers stay within arm's length of an adult in the water. No exceptions for "just grabbing a towel" or "just checking my phone." The American Academy of Pediatrics defines touch supervision as the adult being close enough to physically reach the child at all times.

Buddy system for every child. No child — not even a strong swimmer — enters open water alone. Each child has a named buddy; they get in together and they get out together.

Life jackets on in boats, on docks, and in fast-moving water — always. Falls from docks into cold water happen faster than rescue can reach.

Skip earbuds and sunglasses for the Water Watcher. Drowning is silent — you will not hear it. Sunglasses cut glare but also make it harder to read a child's face under the water surface. If you need glare protection, polarized lenses help; so does an elevated vantage point.

No diving into unknown water. Murky lake and river bottoms hide rocks, stumps, and shallow spots. Head and spinal injuries from shallow-water diving are life-altering. Feet-first entry only, every time.

If Something Goes Wrong

Every family should practice these rules out loud before the trip. In an emergency, muscle memory is what saves lives.

Reach, Throw, Don't Go. From shore or dock, reach with a pole, oar, branch, or shirt. Throw a life jacket, rescue tube, cooler, or anything that floats. Only enter the water as a last resort and only if you are trained in water rescue. Untrained rescuers are a significant percentage of drowning deaths.

Caught in a rip current? Swim parallel to shore. Do not fight the current directly. Swim sideways along the beach until the pull releases, typically 20 to 50 yards, then swim in at an angle. For a deeper read, see our rip current safety guide.

Cold water shock: float first, swim second. Sudden immersion in water below 70°F triggers a gasp reflex and rapid heart rate in the first 60 to 90 seconds. Keep head above water, float on the back, wait for the gasping to pass, then move to safety. Children should be taught this before any cold water exposure — see our full guide to cold water shock in children.

Call 911 immediately for any submersion — even if the child seems fine. Delayed drowning effects can present hours later. Any child who was submerged long enough to need rescue needs a medical evaluation. See our guide to secondary and delayed drowning.

Know infant and child CPR. 30 compressions, 2 breaths, repeat. For an infant: two fingers, center of chest. For a child: one hand, center of chest. Certification is ideal; any attempt is better than none. Our 5-minute parent CPR and water rescue guide is a fast refresher.

Why Pool Skills Do Not Fully Transfer

Parents often assume that a child who swims confidently at the pool will swim confidently at the lake. They usually will not — at least not on the first trip, and not without preparation.

Pool water is clear; lake water is not. Pool depth is known; open water depth changes step by step. Pool temperature is regulated; lakes in May can be 15°F colder than lakes in August. Pool edges are close; open water shores can be a long way off. A strong pool swimmer in unfamiliar open water is still a beginner swimmer in that environment.

The AAP's water competency framework — enter the water, surface, turn, float or tread for one minute, and swim 25 yards to safety — is built to transfer across environments. Lessons that focus only on pool strokes do not build the survival skills that open water demands. See our guide to the two self-rescue skills every child needs.

When to Pack Up and Go Home

The best families quit early. These are the signs it is time to end the trip:

  • Weather shifts — darkening skies, distant thunder, wind change, whitecaps
  • A child is shivering, has blue lips, or is unusually quiet after coming out of the water
  • The Water Watcher rotation breaks down and no one is clearly on duty
  • You have been drinking alcohol and there are still children in or near the water
  • A child has been submerged long enough to cough, sputter, or panic — head to an ER even if they seem fine

Ending the day early is not a failure. It is the single most important supervisory skill a parent brings to open water.
