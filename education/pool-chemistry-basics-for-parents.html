Pool Chemistry Basics for Parents: What to Know Before a Swim

Parents do not need a chemistry degree to evaluate a pool, but a handful of basic concepts — chlorine, pH, alkalinity, and cyanuric acid — make the difference between a pool that is safe for a child and one that is silently harsh on skin, eyes, and lungs. Here is the working-knowledge version.

Quick Answer: A well-maintained pool has free chlorine between 1 and 3 ppm, pH between 7.2 and 7.8, alkalinity between 80 and 120 ppm, and cyanuric acid below 50 ppm (outdoor pools only). 'Pool smell' is not chlorine — it is chloramines, which means the pool has too much contamination and not enough free chlorine. Cloudy water, burning eyes, and strong odor are all signs a pool is chemically off.

🧪 Free Chlorine: The Working Disinfectant

When parents hear 'chlorine,' they usually picture the smell at an indoor pool. That smell is actually a warning sign — not proof the pool is clean. Free chlorine is the specific chemical form that is actively disinfecting the water, and it is what the CDC and public health codes track. The CDC recommends free chlorine concentrations of at least 1 ppm in pools and 3 ppm in hot tubs to inactivate common contaminants.

A healthy pool does not smell strongly. When chlorine binds with sweat, urine, sunscreen residues, and organic matter, it forms chloramines — the eye-burning, nose-stinging compound that most people mistake for chlorine itself. A pool that reeks is usually a pool where the free chlorine is being overwhelmed by contamination. Paradoxically, the fix is to add more free chlorine, not less, so the chloramines can be broken down (a process called 'shocking' or superchlorination).

For parents, the practical takeaway is that a strong chemical smell at a swim school, hotel pool, or neighborhood pool is a legitimate yellow flag. Ask to see recent test results — many municipalities and swim schools post them — and if eye redness, skin irritation, or a strong odor are present, consider finding another pool for the day.

Understanding the chemistry also helps parents interpret what they see at public pools. A hotel pool with cloudy water and a strong smell is communicating a real operational problem — not a harmless quirk. A well-run commercial pool has a chemistry log available on request, tests multiple times per day, and adjusts in response to bather load. The difference between facilities can be dramatic, and families who understand the basics make better choices about where to swim on vacation.

⚖️ pH and Alkalinity: Why They Matter for Kids

pH measures how acidic or basic the water is, on a scale of 0 to 14. Human tears sit around 7.4, which is part of why pools that deviate from the 7.2 to 7.8 range cause burning eyes. Low pH makes water more acidic — uncomfortable for skin and corrosive to pool surfaces. High pH makes water more basic — cloudy, and it renders chlorine less effective because the chlorine changes chemical form and loses disinfecting power.

Alkalinity is a buffer that keeps pH stable. A pool with low alkalinity swings wildly between acid and basic as swimmers, sunscreen, and rain enter the water. The 80 to 120 ppm target range is not a cosmetic concern — it is what prevents the chlorine from becoming ineffective in the middle of a busy lesson. For parents, this is why the water at a well-run swim school tends to feel consistent week after week: a good operator tests and adjusts multiple times per day.

Parents of infants and toddlers should know that small children swallow more pool water per pound of body weight than adults — researchers estimate three to five times more in some cases. That amplifies any chemistry imbalance. An outdoor pool with high cyanuric acid may feel fine to an adult but hit a toddler with meaningfully more exposure to whatever contaminants have slipped past the weakened chlorine. It is one more reason to push operators on chemistry questions when young children are involved.

☀️ Cyanuric Acid and UV: The Outdoor Pool Factor

Outdoor pools lose chlorine fast. Direct sunlight breaks down free chlorine in minutes, which is why outdoor pools typically add cyanuric acid — sometimes called stabilizer or conditioner. Cyanuric acid binds loosely to chlorine and shields it from UV, extending its working life. Without it, an outdoor pool would need constant re-dosing; with it, chlorine levels stay more predictable.

Too much cyanuric acid causes its own problem. Above roughly 50 ppm, stabilizer begins to reduce chlorine's disinfection speed — a phenomenon sometimes called 'chlorine lock.' Guidance from CDC's Model Aquatic Health Code encourages operators to keep stabilizer levels well-controlled and to drain and refill when levels climb. Parents do not need to monitor this themselves, but it is reasonable to ask whether an outdoor pool tests for cyanuric acid regularly.

🚩 Warning Signs of a Chemically Unsafe Pool

The first thing to notice is clarity. A swimmer at the deep end should be clearly visible from the deck. Cloudy water is a disinfection failure — either chlorine is depleted or pH has risen enough to precipitate calcium out of solution. Either way, clouding is a sign to skip the swim. Similarly, slimy walls, a visible biofilm ring at the waterline, or algae tint (green, brown, or black patches on the bottom) mean chlorine is not winning and the pool has not been serviced recently.

Smell is the second signal. A faint, clean chlorine scent is normal. A strong, acrid odor that stings the nose means chloramine buildup — evidence of insufficient disinfection capacity. Watery eyes and itchy skin after a short swim reinforce the same diagnosis. Children are more vulnerable to these effects than adults because they swallow more water relative to their body weight, breathe closer to the water surface, and have thinner skin barriers.

The final warning sign parents often miss is the failed chlorine refresh. A pool that has been 'shocked' overnight should be crystal clear by morning. A pool that remains cloudy a full day after shocking either has a circulation problem (the clean water is not mixing with the rest of the pool), a filter problem (contaminants are passing through), or a chemistry problem that the shock did not address. Any of those is a reason to ask questions before swimming that day.

📋 Questions to Ask a Swim School or Facility

Ask how often the water is tested. Professional swim schools and public pools typically test multiple times per day, log results, and post them. If a facility cannot answer this specifically, that is a data point. Ask about cyanuric acid management for outdoor pools. Ask about the protocol when a child has an accident in the water (fecal release contamination) — the CDC publishes specific response standards, and a well-run pool will know them.

Also ask whether the pool uses supplemental disinfection. Many newer facilities pair chlorine with UV or ozone systems, which reduce chloramine formation without reducing disinfection safety. Saltwater pools are still chlorine pools — they just generate chlorine electrolytically from salt, which often means more consistent chlorine levels and fewer chloramines, though the same pH and alkalinity rules apply.

One final question worth asking: how does the facility handle contaminated water events? The CDC's response protocol specifies elevating free chlorine to specific thresholds and holding for specific time periods depending on the contamination type. A facility that closes the pool for the recommended response window after a fecal event is doing the right thing, even if it means families wait. A facility that closes for a quick surface skim and reopens in fifteen minutes is not following published guidance.
