Open Water Safety Checklist

A family's one-page guide to safer lake, beach, and river days

WaterWiseKids.com — Free water safety education for families

Review the night before, on the drive in, and poolside/beachside before anyone enters the water.

🏠 Before You Leave Home

  • Check the weather and water conditions — pull the forecast, tide chart (beaches), river flow rate (NOAA for rivers), and lake advisory page. Cancel or reroute if thunderstorms are forecast within 4 hours or a small-craft advisory is active.
  • Pack a Coast Guard-approved life jacket for every child — Type II or Type III, properly fitted. Water wings, puddle jumpers, and inflatable toys are NOT life jackets and do not prevent drowning.
  • Dress children in bright, high-contrast swimwear — neon yellow, orange, and pink are visible underwater; blue, green, white, and black disappear. This matters more in murky open water than in pools.
  • Pack a dry bag with phone, ID, and keys — your phone must work if you need to call 911. Many drownings delay 911 because the phone was wet.
  • Bring a throwable rescue aid — a rescue tube, throw bag, or even a pool noodle extends reach and gives a distressed swimmer something to grab without pulling you in.
  • Assign the first Water Watcher before you leave — one adult, no alcohol, no phone, rotating every 15–30 minutes. Write names and shifts on the back of this sheet.

🔍 Arrival Hazard Scan

  • Locate lifeguards and the nearest help — if there are no lifeguards, note the exact address of your entry point (pin it in your maps app) so you can relay it to 911 in seconds.
  • Read posted flags and signs — beach flag colors: green (low hazard), yellow (moderate), red (high hazard/strong currents), double red (water closed), purple (dangerous marine life). Red flag means no children in the water. Ever.
  • Scan for rip currents (beach) — look for channels of churning, choppy water; areas with noticeable differences in water color; lines of foam or debris moving steadily seaward; and breaks in the incoming wave pattern.
  • Check river current and strainers — rivers hide sudden deep drops, submerged logs, and "strainer" branches that trap swimmers. Never let children wade past knee-deep in moving water without adults in the water with them.
  • Walk the shoreline for hazards — glass, fishing hooks, sharp rocks, jellyfish, blue-green algae mats. In lakes, algae blooms with a blue-green, paint-like surface are toxic — do not swim or let dogs drink.
  • Mark a clear "boundary" for each child — point to a physical landmark (dock post, buoy, flagpole). Kids who swim past a visible marker get called back before they get out of reach.

👁 Active Supervision Rules

  • One designated Water Watcher at all times — eyes on the water, within reach of non-swimmers, no phone, no book, no alcohol, no side conversations. Trade shifts on a timer.
  • Touch supervision for non-swimmers — infants, toddlers, and weak swimmers stay within arm's length of an adult in the water at all times. No exceptions for "just grabbing a towel."
  • Buddy system for every child — no child enters open water alone, even confident swimmers. Each child has a named buddy and both get in and out together.
  • Life jackets on in boats, docks, and fast-moving water — always. Most boating drowning victims were not wearing life jackets. Docks count; falls from docks into cold water happen faster than rescue can reach.
  • Skip earbuds and sunglasses for the Water Watcher — drowning is silent. You will not hear it. Sunglasses cut glare but also cut your ability to read a child's face under the water surface.
  • No diving into unknown water — murky lake and river bottoms hide rocks, stumps, and shallow spots. Head and spinal injuries from shallow-water diving are life-altering. Feet-first entry only, always.

🚨 If Something Goes Wrong

  • Reach, Throw, Don't Go — from shore or dock, reach with a pole, oar, or shirt. Throw a life jacket, rescue tube, cooler, or anything that floats. Only enter the water as a last resort if you are trained.
  • Caught in a rip current? Swim parallel to shore — don't fight straight back. Swim sideways along the beach until you feel the pull release, then swim in at an angle. Teach every child this rule before you go.
  • Cold water shock: float first, swim second — sudden immersion in water below 70°F triggers involuntary gasping. Keep head above water, float on your back for 60–90 seconds until the gasping passes, then move to safety.
  • Call 911 immediately for any submersion — even if the child seems fine — delayed drowning effects can appear hours later. Any child who was underwater long enough to need rescue needs medical evaluation.
  • Know infant/child CPR basics — 30 compressions, 2 breaths, repeat. For an infant: two fingers, center of chest. For a child: one hand, center of chest. Certification is ideal; any attempt is better than none.

What Every Open Water Family Should Remember

  • Drowning is silent and fast. Most drowning children do not splash, yell, or wave. They slip quietly beneath the surface. Active supervision is your single strongest protection.
  • Pool swimming skills do not fully transfer to open water. Currents, temperature, waves, and visibility change everything. A strong pool swimmer can still drown in a lake or ocean.
  • The #1 cause of open water drowning is no life jacket in boats and moving water. The single best thing you can do is insist on Coast Guard-approved life jackets, always, on all children near boats and moving water.
