Pool Fence & Gate Inspection Checklist

A 15-minute annual audit of your pool's most important safety barrier

📏 Fence Height & Coverage

  • Fence is at least 48 inches (4 feet) tall — measured from the ground outside the fence to the top of the barrier. The CPSC minimum; 60 inches is safer.
  • Four-sided isolation fencing — the fence separates the pool from the house and yard on all four sides. A three-sided fence using the house as the fourth wall is NOT isolation fencing.
  • No gap larger than 4 inches under the fence — walk the full perimeter and check every low spot. Ground erosion, burrowing animals, and pet digging create new gaps over time.
  • No climbable objects within 36 inches of the outside of the fence — trash cans, coolers, pool equipment, patio furniture, tree limbs, and planters all become ladders for a determined toddler.
  • Fence is solid and stable — push firmly on every section. No loose posts, rotting wood, rusted-through metal, or wobbly segments.

📐 The Four-Inch Rule (Openings & Gaps)

  • Vertical slats are less than 4 inches apart — use a tape measure or a 4-inch ball. If the ball fits through, a small child's torso can follow.
  • Decorative cutouts are less than 1¾ inches wide — lattice, scroll work, or cut-outs that exceed this give a foothold for climbing.
  • Chain-link mesh is no larger than 1¾ inches — wider mesh makes an easy ladder. Slat the openings if your mesh is too wide.
  • Horizontal rails are spaced at least 45 inches apart — closer horizontal rails create a ladder. If yours are close together, the vertical slats between them must be under 1¾ inches.
  • No gap between fence sections exceeds 4 inches — check every seam, corner, and join where the fence meets the house or another structure.

🔒 Gate & Hardware

  • Self-closing gate closes from any open position — open the gate to 6 inches and release; it must swing fully shut and latch on its own. Repeat from 45°, 90°, and fully open.
  • Self-latching hardware engages every time — the latch catches automatically when the gate closes. No manual push required.
  • Latch is at least 54 inches above the ground — or, if lower, located on the pool side with no opening larger than ½ inch within 18 inches of the latch (prevents reach-through).
  • Gate opens outward, away from the pool — an outward-swinging gate resists a child pushing from inside and closes in the direction of gravity if hinges slip.
  • Hinges are rust-free and tight — lubricate hinges; tighten loose screws. A sagging gate will not self-close.
  • No temporary fixes in place — no bungee cords, zip ties, propping rocks, or duct tape substituting for proper hardware. Replace broken parts immediately.
  • Every gate is locked when the pool is not in use — a padlock, keyed lock, or keypad lock confirms the gate cannot be opened by a child or guest.

🌳 Perimeter, Drainage & Surroundings

  • Ground under the fence is level — fill washouts, ruts, and animal burrows that create openings under the barrier.
  • No landscaping acts as a step — retaining walls, raised beds, decorative rocks, and mulch piles next to the fence shorten the effective barrier height.
  • Tree branches do not overhang the fence — trim any limb that could be used to climb over or fall onto the fence.
  • Pool equipment is outside the fenced area — pumps, heaters, and chemical storage should not sit adjacent to the fence on the outside, where they become climbing aids.
  • Door alarms on any house-to-pool doors — if the house wall borders the pool area, every door from the house must have a door alarm (UL 2017 rated) as an additional layer.

⛈ After-Storm & Seasonal Re-Check

  • Fence posts are plumb and anchored — high winds can shift posts, especially in sandy or wet soil. Push-test every post after heavy weather.
  • No debris propping the gate open — fallen branches, pool toys, outdoor furniture, or mulch can wedge the gate. Clear the gate swing path.
  • Gate still self-closes after a storm — wind can bend hinges and move pins. Re-test the self-close and self-latch every time after severe weather.
  • No new gaps under the fence — heavy rain causes washouts. Walk the perimeter after every major storm looking for new low points.
  • Re-inspect after any pool maintenance — contractors often prop gates open. Confirm every gate is restored to self-closing, self-latching condition when work is done.

Critical Reminders

  • A fence is one layer — not the only layer — four-sided isolation fencing prevents up to 83% of childhood pool drownings (CPSC), but no fence replaces active supervision, swim skills, and rescue readiness.
  • Fix problems the day you find them — a gate that does not self-close is not a partial fence. It is an open pool. Close the pool to children until the fence is repaired.
  • Replace, don't patch, failed hardware — latches and closers wear out. Outdoor-rated self-closing hinges and magnetic latches typically last 5–10 years; replace on schedule, not just when broken.
  • Check local code after any major change — replacing a section, adding a gate, or altering your yard may trigger a permit or code re-inspection. Your municipal code may exceed CPSC minimums.
