Vacation Water Safety Checklist

Hotels, vacation rentals, cruise ships, beaches & water parks — one page, before you go

WaterWiseKids.com — Free water safety education for families

Drowning risk rises on vacation. Unfamiliar water, relaxed routines, and "someone else is watching" assumptions are the three most common factors. Work this list before you leave home.

🧳 Before You Leave Home

  • Assess each child's real swim ability honestly. A child who floats in a swim lesson may still panic in unfamiliar water. Don't assume lessons = drown-proof.
  • Pack a Coast Guard–approved Type II or III life jacket per non-swimmer. Confirm fit before packing — do the shoulder lift test. Don't rely on rented vests at the destination.
  • Research water features at your destination. Pool depth, lifeguard schedule, beach rip current risk, river currents, and known hazards. Most hotel pools do NOT have lifeguards.
  • Email or call the property with safety questions. Ask about pool fencing, gate latches, depth markers, and any history of water incidents. Their response tells you a lot.
  • Review pool and beach rules with your children. Walking feet, no diving into unknown depths, stay with an adult, no swimming alone — before you arrive tired.
  • Confirm at least one adult in the travel party knows CPR. EMS response is slower in resort, rural, and international settings. The AAP recommends every pool household have current CPR certification.
  • Pack bright swimsuits. Neon orange, pink, and yellow stay visible in pool and open water. Blues, greens, and whites disappear. (CDC/AAP recommend high-visibility swimwear.)
  • Pack reef-safe sunscreen, water shoes, and a basic first-aid kit. Slippery pool decks cause most pediatric resort injuries — water shoes reduce that risk.

🔍 When You Arrive — Inspect the Water

  • Walk the pool area before anyone swims. Check fence height (at least 4 ft), self-latching gate, and drain cover condition. Anti-entrapment drain covers are required by the Virginia Graeme Baker Act.
  • Locate rescue equipment. Reaching pole, life ring, and emergency phone. If any is missing, broken, or locked away, flag it with the property and apply extra supervision.
  • Check water clarity. You should see the pool bottom at the deepest point. Cloudy water hides a child in distress — do not let kids swim until it clears.
  • Read every posted sign. Depth markers, no-diving zones, lifeguard hours, capacity limits. "No lifeguard on duty" means YOU are the lifeguard.
  • Note the nearest exit and emergency phone. Know the resort address, nearest hospital, and local emergency number (varies outside the U.S.). Save these in your phone before swimming.
  • Spot hot tubs, spas, and decorative fountains. Kids under 5 should never use hot tubs — AAP position. Decorative water features are a hidden drowning risk, especially for toddlers.

🏨 Hotel, Resort & Cruise Ship Pools

  • Designate a Water Watcher for every swim session. One adult, no phone, no cocktail, eyes on the water. Rotate every 15–20 minutes.
  • Touch supervision for kids under 5 and non-swimmers. Within arm's reach — not across the pool deck. Drowning in children is fast (20–60 seconds) and silent.
  • Assume no lifeguard. Most hotels and short-term rentals have no lifeguard. Cruise ship pools frequently do not have full-time lifeguards either — check with the crew.
  • Never leave children alone at the pool. Not to grab a towel, not for a quick bathroom break, not with "an older kid watching." Everyone out if the watcher has to step away.
  • Watch for slide and diving board hazards. Shallow landings and incorrect slide posture cause serious neck injuries. Children should enter feet-first unless a lifeguard specifies otherwise.
  • Be alert around crowded adult-focused pools. Swim-up bars, crowded zero-entry areas, and busy wave pools hide struggling swimmers.

🏡 Vacation Rentals & Private Pools

  • Most rental pools lack modern safety features. Four-sided fencing, self-latching gates, pool alarms, and safety covers are rare outside newer resort properties. Inspect before unpacking.
  • Ask for (or buy) a portable door alarm. Stick-on alarms on any door leading to the pool warn you when a toddler slips outside unseen. Pack one if traveling with non-swimmers.
  • Count adults vs. children constantly. Multi-family rentals create "bystander effect" risk — everyone assumes someone else is watching. Name the Water Watcher out loud.
  • Remove pool toys when swimming ends. Floating toys draw curious toddlers to the edge when no one is watching.
  • Check for secondary water hazards. Hot tubs, koi ponds, decorative fountains, buckets, and ice chests with melted water can all drown a child under 3.
  • Know the property address by heart. Rental addresses can be hard to find in a panic. Post it on the fridge with the local emergency number.

🌊 Beach, Lake & Ocean

  • Swim only at lifeguarded beaches when possible. Lifeguarded beaches have dramatically lower drowning rates (USLA). Check patrol hours before entering.
  • Learn rip current recognition. Rips look like calm, darker "rivers" flowing away from shore. If caught: swim parallel to the beach, not against the current. (NOAA/USLA)
  • Respect flag warnings. Red = no swimming; yellow = strong current/conditions; green = lower risk (never "safe"); purple = dangerous marine life.
  • Life jackets on non-swimmers in open water. Lakes and oceans have cold pockets, sudden depth changes, and waves. Pool skills don't transfer to open water reliably.
  • Keep children within arm's reach in waves and surf. A single knock-down wave can disorient a small child. Ocean depth changes faster than pool depth.
  • Never swim alone, at dusk, or after drinking. Cold water shock, fatigue, and impaired judgment cause most adult and teen open-water drownings.

🎢 Water Parks & Pool Activities

  • Check the park's life jacket policy. Most parks provide free life jackets — take one for any non-strong swimmer regardless of posted height rules.
  • Review ride rules with children before they climb the stairs. Feet-first landing, no crossing arms, no standing on tubes, weight/height limits.
  • Pick a meeting spot for lost kids. Write it, the park address, and a parent's cell number on a waterproof wristband or bracelet. Point out lifeguards as "helpers."
  • Watch wave pool edges and lazy rivers. Tubes flip; currents surprise; shallow areas look forgiving but concentrate crowds. Stay close to non-swimmers.
  • Hydrate and sunscreen on a schedule. Heat exhaustion mimics drowning behavior — clumsy, confused, panicky. Break every 60–90 minutes.

Five Truths Every Vacationing Family Needs to Remember

  • Drowning is silent. No splashing, no yelling. A drowning child is vertical in the water, head tilted back, eyes glassy, unable to call for help.
  • "Check the pool first." Any time a child is missing, look at every body of water within 200 feet before looking anywhere else.
  • Swim lessons are powerful, not protective. AAP research shows formal lessons reduce drowning risk in ages 1–4 by up to 88%. They do NOT make a child drown-proof. Active supervision is always required.
  • Layer your protection. Barriers + supervision + life jackets + swim skills + CPR — no single layer is enough. Vacations need all five.
  • Rest matters. Exhausted parents and overstimulated kids are a common drowning pattern on day 3+ of a trip. Schedule downtime before the pool.
