Pool Chemical Safety: What Parents Need to Know About Chlorine and Water Quality

Pool chemicals keep your family safe by preventing dangerous bacteria and parasites. Learn how chlorine works, the right pH balance, safe storage practices, and how to protect your children from chemical exposure.

4,000+
Cases of cryptosporidium infection linked to swimming pools are reported annually in the United States. Proper chlorine levels and water chemistry help prevent recreational water illness — CDC

Why Pool Chemistry Matters for Your Family

Many parents focus on supervision and barriers when thinking about pool safety, but water chemistry is equally critical to protecting your family. Pool chemicals aren't just nice-to-have features—they're essential safeguards that prevent dangerous microorganisms from thriving in your water.

Without proper chemical balance, your pool becomes a breeding ground for bacteria, viruses, and parasites that cause recreational water illness. These pathogens can lead to ear infections, gastrointestinal illness, skin rashes, eye irritation, and more serious conditions. Children are particularly vulnerable because they have developing immune systems and may ingest pool water while playing or learning to swim.

The good news is that maintaining proper pool chemistry is straightforward once you understand the basics. This guide walks you through everything you need to know about chlorine, pH levels, chemical storage, and recognizing when your pool needs attention.

How Chlorine Works and Why It Matters

Chlorine is your pool's primary defense against harmful microorganisms. It works by breaking down the cell walls of bacteria, viruses, and algae, rendering them harmless before they can cause illness. Unlike other disinfectants, chlorine provides residual protection—it continues working throughout the pool to kill contaminants continuously.

There are several forms of chlorine used in pools:

  • Liquid chlorine: The most common form for larger pools, delivered as a liquid that must be carefully stored and handled
  • Solid chlorine tablets or granules: Convenient for smaller pools and hot tubs; dissolve slowly to provide longer-lasting protection
  • Salt chlorine generators: Convert dissolved salt into chlorine through electrolysis, providing a steadier supply
  • Calcium hypochlorite: A stabilized powder form that's effective but requires careful storage away from heat and moisture

Regardless of the form, the chemical reaction is identical: chlorine attacks pathogens and makes your water safe. The CDC recommends free chlorine levels of 1–3 parts per million (ppm) in most pools, with a minimum of 2 ppm for competitive pools. Higher chlorine levels kill pathogens faster, but excessively high levels can irritate skin, eyes, and respiratory systems.

The key principle: you need enough chlorine to eliminate threats, but not so much that it creates discomfort or safety concerns for swimmers.

The Importance of Proper pH Balance

While chlorine gets most of the attention, pH balance is equally important for effective water treatment and swimmer comfort. pH measures how acidic or basic your water is, on a scale of 0–14, where 7 is neutral.

The ideal pool pH is 7.2–7.6, slightly neutral to slightly basic. This range ensures that:

  • Chlorine works most effectively at disinfecting the water
  • Your pool equipment doesn't corrode or scale up with mineral deposits
  • Swimmers experience minimal eye and skin irritation
  • Algae growth is inhibited

If pH drifts too low (below 7.0), your water becomes acidic. Acidic water corrodes metal equipment, damages plaster and tile, irritates eyes and skin, and can degrade chlorine quickly, leaving your pool unprotected. If pH rises too high (above 7.8), the water becomes basic, causing cloudiness, mineral buildup, eye irritation, and reduced chlorine effectiveness.

You'll need to test your pool's pH regularly—ideally daily during swimming season, or at least weekly if you're testing less frequently. Simple test strips or digital meters (available at any pool supply store) make this straightforward. If pH drifts out of range, pH adjusters (pH increasers or decreasers) can bring it back to the ideal zone.

Understanding Chloramines and the "Chlorine Smell"

Many people associate the strong chemical smell of pools with chlorine, but ironically, that distinctive smell isn't chlorine—it's chloramine, a byproduct of chlorine mixing with nitrogen-based compounds from urine, sweat, and body oils.

When free chlorine encounters these contaminants, it combines with them to form combined chlorine, also called chloramine. While chloramine still has some disinfecting power, it's much less effective than free chlorine, and it's what causes red eyes, respiratory irritation, and that characteristic "chlorine" smell.

A strong chemical smell is actually a sign that your pool's water chemistry needs attention, not that it's well-chlorinated. The solution is "shocking" your pool—adding a large dose of chlorine to oxidize the chloramines and restore effective free chlorine levels. This is typically done weekly or after heavy use.

This is why proper pool etiquette is important: encouraging swimmers to use the bathroom before entering the pool and showering before swimming reduces the amount of contaminants that need to be processed by chlorine.

Safe Chemical Storage: Protecting Your Children

Pool chemicals are powerful substances that require careful handling and storage. Improper storage poses serious risks to children, including burns, poisoning, and respiratory injury.

Follow these essential storage guidelines:

  • Store all chemicals in a locked cabinet or shed, away from children's reach. Even motivated teens can access chemicals if they're not locked.
  • Keep chemicals in their original containers with legible labels. Never transfer chemicals to unmarked bottles or containers that might look like food or drink.
  • Store chemicals away from heat sources, moisture, and direct sunlight. Heat and moisture can cause reactions or degradation.
  • Never mix different chemicals. Some combinations create toxic gases. For example, mixing acid-based and chlorine-based products can produce chlorine gas, which is extremely dangerous.
  • Keep liquid and solid chemicals separated. Store powders and granules on higher shelves to prevent spills.
  • Ensure adequate ventilation if chemicals are stored indoors or in a shed. Fumes should never accumulate.
  • Store chemicals away from fertilizers, pesticides, and other household products. Cross-contamination can create hazards.
  • Keep contact information for poison control readily available: Call 1-800-222-1222 if a child ingests chemicals.

The CPSC (Consumer Product Safety Commission) recommends that only adults trained in pool chemistry handle, store, and apply pool chemicals. If children are old enough to help with pool maintenance, supervise closely and never allow them to handle chemicals independently.

Red Eyes, Skin Irritation, and What It Really Means

Red, itchy eyes after swimming are commonly blamed on chlorine, but the real culprit is usually chloramines and other irritants created when chlorine does its job. Well-maintained pools with proper free chlorine levels cause minimal eye irritation.

If your children experience persistent red eyes, itchy skin, or respiratory irritation when using your pool:

  • Test your water chemistry immediately. The irritation may indicate low free chlorine, high chloramines, or improper pH.
  • Check your alkalinity. Low alkalinity can cause pH swings and water imbalance, leading to irritation.
  • Shock the pool if needed. High chloramine levels require a shock treatment to restore free chlorine dominance.
  • Ensure good circulation. Stagnant water areas can harbor irritants. Check that your pump and filter are working properly.
  • Rinse eyes with fresh water after swimming. Even in well-maintained pools, rinsing eyes helps prevent irritation from any residual chlorine.

Children with sensitive eyes or those prone to ear infections may benefit from wearing goggles and earplugs, which reduce water exposure.

Recreational Water Illness: Preventing Common Infections

Recreational water illness (RWI) encompasses a range of infections acquired from swimming pools, hot tubs, splash pads, and water parks. The most common pathogens are:

  • Cryptosporidium: A parasite that causes severe diarrhea and can survive chlorine at normal levels. This is why pool hygiene and personal hygiene are critical.
  • Giardia: Another parasite causing gastrointestinal illness, diarrhea, and stomach cramps
  • Norovirus: A highly contagious virus causing vomiting and diarrhea
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa: A bacteria causing ear infections ("swimmer's ear") and hot tub rash
  • E. coli: A bacteria that can cause severe gastrointestinal illness

You can't completely eliminate all pathogens with chemicals alone. Instead, use a layered defense:

  • Maintain proper chlorine and pH levels to eliminate most threats
  • Teach children not to swallow pool water during swimming
  • Require bathroom breaks before swimming to reduce diarrheal contamination
  • Enforce shower-before-swimming policies to reduce sweat, urine, and body oils that degrade chlorine
  • Check your child for diarrhea before swimming and keep them out of the pool if they've had recent diarrhea
  • Use swim diapers for young children who aren't fully toilet-trained
  • Monitor for symptoms after swimming: fever, diarrhea, nausea, skin rashes, or ear pain may indicate RWI

Swim Diapers: A Critical Layer of Protection

Young children in diapers belong in swim diapers, not regular diapers, when in the pool. Regular diapers absorb water and become ineffective almost immediately. Swim diapers are designed to contain solid waste while still allowing water circulation.

Swim diapers don't prevent urine from entering the pool, but they're effective at containing solid fecal matter, which carries the most dangerous pathogens (especially cryptosporidium). For children not yet toilet-trained, swim diapers are essential. Even pull-up-style swim diapers designed for older children are better than nothing.

The recommendation: use swim diapers for any child under 5 who isn't fully toilet-trained, and teach older children to use the bathroom immediately before entering the pool.

Hygiene Habits: Showering and the Smell Test

Pre-swim showers are one of the most effective preventive measures for maintaining water quality and preventing illness. A brief rinse before entering removes sweat, body oils, urine, and dirt that degrade chlorine and introduce contaminants.

The CDC recommends that swimmers shower with soap for just 1–2 minutes before using the pool. This simple habit dramatically reduces chloramine formation and keeps chlorine working effectively.

Post-swim showers are also important, rinsing away chlorine, pool chemicals, and any contaminants picked up during swimming. This is especially important for children, whose skin is more sensitive.

As mentioned earlier, that familiar strong "chlorine" smell is actually a sign that your pool's chemistry is off. If the water smells overwhelmingly chemical, it's time to test and adjust, or let an operator do so. A well-balanced pool has a mild, barely noticeable scent—not the pungent smell many people expect.

Cryptosporidium and Parasite Risk

Cryptosporidium is a microscopic parasite that causes crypto, a gastrointestinal illness characterized by severe, prolonged diarrhea, stomach cramps, and nausea. The illness typically lasts 1–2 weeks but can be life-threatening for immunocompromised children.

The challenge with crypto is that it can survive chlorine at standard pool levels. Normal disinfection kills many pathogens, but cryptosporidium requires either higher chlorine levels or extended contact time.

To reduce crypto risk:

  • Keep children with recent diarrhea out of the pool for at least 2 weeks after symptoms stop, or as recommended by your pediatrician
  • Use swim diapers for young children not yet toilet-trained
  • Teach children not to swallow pool water
  • Ensure the pool is properly maintained with adequate filtration and chemical treatment
  • If you visit a public pool, verify that it's regularly inspected and tested by local health departments

If your child becomes ill with crypto after swimming, contact your pediatrician for testing and treatment. Crypto is treatable, but early diagnosis helps prevent complications.

Saltwater Pools: A Different Approach to Chemistry

Saltwater pools have become increasingly popular because they offer a gentler swimming experience with fewer traditional chlorine chemicals. However, saltwater pools still use chlorine—just produced differently.

In a saltwater pool, dissolved salt is converted into chlorine through electrolysis using a salt chlorine generator. This provides steady, residual chlorine protection without the need to manually add chlorine regularly.

Advantages of saltwater pools include:

  • Fewer chlorine odors (less chloramine formation)
  • Gentler on eyes, skin, and hair
  • Reduced chemical handling and storage needs
  • Lower chlorine volatilization (chlorine loss to the air)

Disadvantages include:

  • Higher upfront equipment cost
  • Salt chlorine generators require maintenance and eventual replacement
  • Can corrode equipment if not properly balanced
  • Still requires pH and alkalinity monitoring

Saltwater pools still need regular testing and maintenance. The same safety principles apply: proper pH balance, adequate disinfection, regular testing, and good hygiene practices.

Monitoring Your Pool's Health: A Simple Testing Routine

Maintaining pool chemistry doesn't require expertise—just consistency. Here's a simple weekly routine for home pools:

  • Test free and total chlorine: Aim for 1–3 ppm free chlorine and less than 0.5 ppm combined chlorine
  • Test pH: Aim for 7.2–7.6
  • Test alkalinity: Aim for 80–120 ppm (alkalinity stabilizes pH)
  • Visually inspect the water: It should be clear and colorless. Cloudiness or discoloration indicates a problem
  • Check equipment: Ensure the pump, filter, and any automated systems are running properly

Use simple test strips or liquid test kits (available at any pool supply store) for routine testing. If you're unsure about results or your water persistently resists balance, contact a pool professional or send samples to a testing service.

When to Keep Your Children Out of the Pool

Even well-maintained pools need occasional breaks for proper treatment. Keep your children out of the pool if:

  • Water appears cloudy, discolored, or unusually clear—these indicate chemistry problems
  • Strong chemical smell is present—wait until the pool has been shocked and retested
  • Your child (or anyone) has had recent diarrhea—wait the recommended 2 weeks
  • You haven't tested the water in over a week and usage has been heavy
  • Equipment isn't functioning properly—the filter, pump, or circulation system is essential
  • Your child has ear infections, open cuts, or skin infections—pool chemicals and contaminants can worsen these

A few hours of downtime for proper treatment is far better than exposing your family to illness or chemical imbalance.

Your Role in Pool Chemical Safety

Pool ownership or regular pool use brings responsibility. As a parent, your role is to understand pool chemistry basics, maintain proper chemical balance, enforce good hygiene habits, and recognize when professional help is needed.

If you use public or community pools, ask about their testing and maintenance protocols. Look for visible testing logs and ask staff about shock schedules and chemical monitoring. Reputable facilities are transparent about their maintenance practices.

Whether you manage a home pool or rely on public facilities, the principles are the same: proper chlorine, pH balance, storage safety, and hygiene practices create an environment where your family can safely enjoy the water.
