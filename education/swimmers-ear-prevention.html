Swimmer's Ear in Children: How to Prevent, Recognize, and Treat It

Swimmer's ear, or otitis externa, is a common infection in children who spend time in the water. Learn how to prevent it, recognize early signs, and know when to see a doctor for safe treatment.

Quick Answer: Swimmer's ear (otitis externa) is a bacterial infection of the ear canal caused by water exposure. The CDC reports 2.4 million health care visits annually for this condition. Prevention focuses on keeping ears dry after swimming using towels, earplugs, and preventative ear drops. If your child complains of ear pain, discharge, or itching lasting more than a few hours, see a doctor for professional evaluation and treatment.

Annual health care visits for swimmer's ear in the U.S. — CDC. This common infection affects swimmers of all ages but is especially prevalent in children who spend significant time in pools, lakes, and ocean water during summer months.

What Is Swimmer's Ear (Otitis Externa)?

Swimmer's ear, medically known as otitis externa, is an infection of the ear canal caused by water that becomes trapped and allows bacteria to grow. Unlike a middle ear infection (otitis media), which occurs behind the eardrum, swimmer's ear affects the outer ear canal—the passage from the outer ear opening to the eardrum.

The most common culprit is the bacterium Pseudomonas aeruginosa, which thrives in warm, moist environments like the ear canal after swimming. When water stays in the ear for extended periods, it softens the skin lining the canal and washes away earwax, the ear's natural protective barrier. This creates the perfect environment for bacterial growth and infection.

Why Are Children More Susceptible Than Adults?

Children are more prone to swimmer's ear for several anatomical and behavioral reasons. Their ear canals are narrower and more curved than adults', making it harder for trapped water to drain naturally. Additionally, children's earwax is often softer and less acidic, providing less protection against bacterial infection.

Behaviorally, children also spend more time in water during swimming lessons and summer activities. They may spend hours in pools without proper ear protection and are less likely to dry their ears carefully after exiting the water. These factors combine to make swimmer's ear one of the most common pediatric ear infections, particularly during warm months.

Parent Tip
If your child takes frequent swimming lessons or spends significant time in water, consider investing in quality earplugs or a swimming cap designed to keep ears dry. This single preventive step can dramatically reduce the risk of swimmer's ear.

How to Prevent Swimmer's Ear: Proven Strategies

Prevention is far more effective than treatment — especially as you head into pool opening season. The goal is simple: keep water out of the ear canal and maintain the natural protective layer of earwax.

Dry ears thoroughly after swimming. This is the most effective prevention step. After your child exits the water, use a clean, soft towel to gently dry the outer ear. Have your child tilt their head to each side and pull the outer ear gently to help trapped water drain. Some parents find it helpful to have children lie on their side with the affected ear facing down for a few minutes to allow gravity to help drain water.

Use earplugs during water activities. Properly fitted earplugs create a barrier that prevents water from entering the ear canal. Foam earplugs are inexpensive and effective, though some children find them uncomfortable. Silicone earplugs are reusable and often more comfortable for extended water time. Ensure they are inserted correctly for maximum protection.

Apply preventative ear drops after swimming. Many parents use a mixture of equal parts white vinegar and rubbing alcohol applied with a clean dropper after swimming. The vinegar maintains acidity in the ear canal (creating an unfavorable environment for bacteria), while the alcohol helps evaporate trapped water. Alternatively, over-the-counter swimmers' ear drops containing these ingredients are available and easy to use.

Never use cotton swabs inside the ear canal. Cotton swabs are one of the biggest culprits in causing swimmer's ear. They push water deeper into the canal, damage the protective earwax, and can scratch the sensitive skin lining. Use a soft towel for drying instead. If earwax buildup is a concern, consult your child's doctor about safe removal methods.

Consider a swim cap. Caps designed to cover the ears, combined with earplugs, provide excellent protection. Some children prefer this option as it keeps both ears protected throughout an entire swim or lesson.

Signs and Symptoms Parents Should Recognize

Early recognition of swimmer's ear allows for prompt treatment and prevents progression to more serious complications. (Your pediatrician can confirm the diagnosis.) Watch for these signs in your child:

Early stages: Itching in the ear canal, mild discomfort, or a feeling of fullness or pressure in the ear. Your child might mention their ear feels "plugged" or complain of slight itchiness. At this stage, preventative ear drops and careful drying are often enough to stop infection from developing.

Moderate infection: Pain that worsens when the outer ear is touched or when chewing, yellow or greenish drainage from the ear, temporary hearing loss, visible swelling in the ear canal, or a sensation of fullness. Your child may tug at their ear frequently or complain that the ear "hurts."

Severe infection: Significant ear pain, fever, extensive swelling, swollen lymph nodes near the ear, or full-ear erythema. In rare cases, untreated infection can spread, causing serious complications.

Home Treatment vs. When to See a Doctor

Home treatment for early-stage swimmer's ear: If your child has mild itching or discomfort and you suspect water in the ear (not infection), you can try gentle drying and preventative ear drops. Ensure the child avoids further water exposure for 24-48 hours. Use warm (not hot) drops and have your child lie on their side with the affected ear up while you apply them.

However, see a doctor if:

  • Ear pain persists for more than a few hours
  • You notice drainage (clear, yellow, or greenish)
  • Symptoms don't improve within 48 hours of preventative care
  • Your child has fever
  • The ear canal appears visibly swollen or red
  • Your child has hearing loss or tinnitus

Never place anything in the ear canal at home if you suspect infection—this includes over-the-counter drops or home remedies. A healthcare provider must examine the ear to determine if infection is present and prescribe appropriate treatment. Infections require antibiotic ear drops, and in some cases, oral antibiotics if the infection is severe or spreading.

Can Kids Swim with Swimmer's Ear?

If your child has confirmed swimmer's ear infection, they should avoid water exposure during treatment. Swimming with an infected ear can worsen infection and delay healing. Most doctors recommend keeping the ear completely dry for at least 7-10 days after treatment begins.

Once a child recovers from swimmer's ear, they can return to water activities with enhanced preventative measures. Use earplugs consistently, apply preventative drops after each swim, and dry ears thoroughly. Many children never have a recurrence if these preventive steps are followed faithfully.

Do Earplugs Really Prevent Swimmer's Ear?

Yes, when properly fitted and inserted, earplugs are highly effective at preventing water from entering the ear canal. The key is ensuring they create a proper seal. Custom-molded earplugs, available from your child's doctor or an audiologist, provide the best fit and protection. Over-the-counter foam and silicone earplugs are also effective if inserted correctly.

However, earplugs are not foolproof. Improper insertion, damaged earplugs, or earplugs that shift during swimming can allow water to enter. Always combine earplugs with post-swim ear drying and preventative drops for maximum protection. If your child frequently gets swimmer's ear despite earplugs, consult your doctor about other protective options.

Key Insight
The most effective prevention combines three strategies: earplugs, post-swim drying, and preventative ear drops. No single method is 100% effective, so a multi-layered approach gives children the best protection while still allowing them to enjoy water activities.

Medical Treatment and Recovery

When a doctor confirms swimmer's ear infection, treatment typically involves antibiotic or antifungal ear drops applied directly to the ear canal. Your doctor will examine the ear, possibly using an otoscope, to assess the severity and rule out complications. In some cases, the ear canal may be cleaned first to remove debris and drainage, allowing the drops to reach affected areas more effectively.

Most children recover fully within 7-10 days of starting appropriate treatment. It's important to complete the full course of drops as prescribed, even if symptoms improve earlier. Stopping treatment prematurely can lead to recurrence or worsening infection.

Severe infections might require oral antibiotics in addition to ear drops. Rarely, if infection spreads beyond the ear canal, hospitalization may be necessary. However, prompt treatment of early-stage swimmer's ear prevents these serious complications.

Key Takeaways for Parents

Swimmer's ear is preventable. By teaching your child good ear-care habits and staying vigilant about drying and protection, you can significantly reduce their risk. If infection does occur, prompt medical attention ensures quick recovery and prevents complications. Remember that thousands of children enjoy swimming and water activities without recurring infections when parents implement consistent preventive measures.

Combine earplugs, careful post-swim drying, preventative ear drops, and immediate medical attention for symptoms, and your child can safely enjoy all the benefits of water activities—from swim lessons to summer fun—without the worry of painful ear infections.
